In this edition of Relevant & Irreverent, we're cheering a historic victory for women in Sudan and a kindness by a doctor in India. We're also talking about the place of gods in modern culture and Google's very own Rosetta stone.

In the spotlight

Image used for representational purpose only.

One giant leap for (wo)mankind: Sudan has given back its women a basic human right. On Saturday, Justice Minister Nasredeen Abdulbari announced that the country is banning female genital mutilation, a barbaric practice that leads to lifelong physical and psychological trauma for the woman. In another hurrah for women's rights, the north African country will no longer require women to obtain permission from a male member of the family to travel with her children. And, in another reversal of almost 40 years of hardline Islamist policies, non-Muslims, some 3% of the country's population, will be allowed to consume alcohol in private. Drinking is still prohibited for Muslims, with flogging the punishment meted out to offenders. However, apostasy will no longer be treated as a criminal act, leaving Muslims in the country free to convert if they so choose. In 1983, the country's then president, Jaafar Nimeiri, introduced Islamic law and banned alcohol, throwing bottles of whiskey into the Nile as a symbolic gesture. When the autocrat Omar al-Bashir came to power in 1989, he strengthened Islamic law, further restricting citizens' freedoms. In 2019, after 30 years at the helm, Bashir was overthrown and the new transition government, led by Abdalla Hamdok, promised to bring democracy to the country.
What once was repulsive, is now all good: Although Pakistan has come down hard on acid attacks and the country's new legislation imposes hefty fines and long prison terms on offenders, women in the South Asian country often still fall victim to the heinous crime. Scarred and disfigured, these women find it hard to survive in a society that rejects them and treats them as outcasts. Pakistani businesswoman Masarrat Misbah has sought to mitigate that situation. In 1980, she launched Depilex – a salon that would become a haven for victims of acid attacks – and 25 years later she set up the Depilex Smileagain Foundation. Over the years, several battered women have found their way to Depilex, where they are offered counselling, reconstructive surgery, vocational training and jobs. Margaret Heera is one such survivor. In 2013, after two years of marriage, her husband, unhappy about the small dowry she brought him, poured acid on her. Today Heera is a beautician at Depilex's Lahore branch. She says that although clients at first were "shocked" when they saw her scars and refused to allow her to work for them, perspectives eventually changed and things are "all good" now.

Stand up for your gods

A god among rockstars. With the release of their latest music video, K-pop group Blackpink went on to set multiple Guinness World Records, including for most viewed YouTube video in 24 hours. They also went on to earn the ire of many of their Indian fans for using an idol of Lord Ganesha in the video. The use of the statue of the elephant-headed Hindu god, placed on the ground next to an Aladdin lamp, in the single "How You Like That" has drawn criticism from fans, with many accusing the all-girl Korean band of cultural appropriation and of defiling the idol by placing it on the ground. Blackpink were quick to respond and Ganesha was soon gone from the video.

This Bud's for Brahma. Unlike Blackpink and their disappearing Ganesha, Anheuser-Busch is taking a tougher stance. A group comprising representatives of the Christian, Jewish, Buddhist, Hindu and Jain faiths recently called on the Belgium-based brewer to rename its Brahma line of beers, brewed and loved in Brazil since 1888. In the words of group spokesperson Rajan Zed, Brahma, the three-headed Hindu god, is not meant to be a "toasting tool" and Anheuser-Busch should just call the beer by another name. However, the makers of Budweiser begged to differ, with a company representative noting that while Hindus are a tiny minority in Brazil, the Brahma brand is "very important to the culture of the country." He did clarify though that the beers were named after Englishman Joseph Bramah, who is credited with inventing the draft pump valve, and not in honor of the Hindu god, who is credited with creating the world. To make the name work better in Portuguese, the spelling was changed from Bramah to Brahma. We're staying non-controversial (a rose by any other name and so on) and toasting with Budweiser.

Meanwhile

Plastic, not at all fantastic: About 50 tonnes of plastic waste washed ashore a 10-km stretch of Cox's Bazar beach late on Saturday and carcasses of at least 20 Olive Ridley turtles were spotted among the waste the next morning. With many more turtles entangled in the waste, locals worked frenetically to free them and release them back into the sea. According to Bangladesh's forestry department, never before has such a large quantity of plastic washed ashore the 120-km beach along the Bay of Bengal. Said Moazzem Hossain of Save the Nature of Bangladesh: "This is a unique case of plastic invasion. It sends a great danger signal to our marine biodiversity." Meanwhile in Kaziranga National Park in neighboring Assam in India, more than 50 wild animals have died due to floods in the area, with 95% of the 430 square-km park submerged under water. Kaziranga is home to India's famous one-horned rhino.

A death-defying dolphin:A group of innovators in New Zealand is working alongside creators of some of Hollywood's most recognizable creatures to bring to an aquarium near you an animatronic dolphin. In recent times ethical concerns over keeping wild animals in captivity have been mounting, so have costs to marine parks of acquiring and maintaining live animals. "Yet the audience appetite for this type of entertainment and education has remained constant," says Edge Innovations, the New Zealand-based group working to create an alternative to the real thing. Each robot dolphin weighs 270 kgs and can be made to perform the same tricks humans have come to expect from dolphins in zoos. In fact, Edge says, a test audience was unable to tell that the dolphin they interacted with was a robot. If you want one, it'll set you back some NZ$40 million ($26 million).
Above and beyond: At a time when reports are emerging in India about the mishandling of bodies of Covid-19 victims, a doctor in the state of Telangana has warmed hearts with his compassionate handling of a death. When Peddapalli government hospital recorded its first Covid-19 death on Sunday, Pendyala Sriram, a district medical surveillance officer, was called in. In the absence of an ambulance, municipal authorities sent a tractor to take the body to the cremation ground. However, on learning of his assignment, the tractor driver refused to drive the vehicle. Anticipating the distress of the victim's family at having to wait to perform the last rites, the 45-year-old doctor donned a PPE suit and drove the tractor to the graveyard himself. We salute Dr. Pendyala Sriram for his humanity.

A gin and tonic like Royalty does it: Gin lovers in the UK can this summer get a taste of Buckingham Palace in their favorite gin cocktail. The Royal Collection Trust this week launched a small-batch London dry gin made with 12 botanicals, several of which come from the Queen's garden. Here's the recipe from the sellers: "For the perfect thirst-quencher, the recommended serving method is to pour a measure of the gin into an ice-filled short tumbler before topping up with tonic and garnishing with a slice of lemon." Each 700 ml bottle of Buckingham Palace gin is priced at £40, a bargain given that this is the stuff that's going to be served at official Palace events.

Homeward bound on a brand-new bicycle: When the coronavirus sent Europe into lockdown, University of Aberdeen student Kleon Papadimitriou realized he wasn't going to be able to get a flight back home to Athens. By the first of April, most of his friends had left and Papadimitriou wasn't exactly excited at the prospect of having to spend the next month or more in quarantine in Scotland. That's when the idea of biking the 4,000-odd kms between Scotland and Greece began to take shape and on May 10, a journey that was initially just a "pipe dream," formally kicked off. Armed with a sleeping bag, canned food, peanut butter and bread, the 20-year-old set off on his new bike for home. Most nights of his 48-day journey were spent camping in fields and forests, although the prospect of a bed and hot shower did occasionally push the introverted Papadimitriou to seek out friends and acquaintances along the way. "You will learn things about yourself and you will surprise yourself," is what Papadimitriou had to say of the experience.
Google's very own Rosetta stone: Google this week launched Fabricius, a hieroglyphics translator that uses Artificial Intelligence to decode ancient languages. The feature, added to Google's Arts & Culture app, allows users to convert their words and emojis into shareable hieroglyphs. But Fabricius is much more than a new fun way to chat with friends, it's also for the serious student of Egyptology. Users can upload pictures of real hieroglyphs onto the software's Workbench tool, which can then be used to digitally enhance the images to allow for closer analysis. Users can also trace an outline of hieroglyphics and use the software's database and machine learning capabilities to attempt to decipher it. The tech giant is hopeful that the more people use the tool, the more extensive and refined its database will become.

Iceland wants your lockdown screams: The Nordic nation is asking people to let it all out. A campaign Promote Iceland is running on its website is inviting people to record their screams via the website and have it released into the picturesque Icelandic landscape on loudspeakers set up across the country. "You've been through a lot this year and it looks like you need the perfect place to let your frustrations out. … It looks like you need Iceland," the organizers say. For the uninitiated, the website offers tips on the best stance to adopt while letting it rip; and if listening to others scream will help you get rid of any inhibitions, you can do that, too. Have a go on the website for now and "when you're ready, come let it out for real. You'll feel better, we promise."

4 July 2020

In this edition of Relevant & Irreverent, we're talking about one country's move to convert a museum into a mosque and another's seeming indifference to dying pachyderms. We're also exploring the world of green cereal and giving thanks to those doomsday movies.
In the spotlight
Turkey's monumental move: President Recep Tayyip Erdogan's move to convert the world-famous Hagia Sophia tourist attraction into a mosque is aimed at appeasing his voters, but it's finding opponents all over the world. Hagia Sophia was built in the sixth century during the Christian Byzantine Empire and served as the seat of the Greek Orthodox Church. When the Ottomans conquered Istanbul in the 15th century, the cathedral was immediately converted into a mosque. In 1935, Mustafa Kemal Ataturk, the founder of the Turkish Republic, to proclaim Turkey's secular nature to the world, turned the monument into a museum. On Thursday, the country's highest administrative court heard a case challenging the legality of Ataturk's move. Although there have long been calls to convert the UNESCO World Heritage site back into a mosque to better reflect the country's identity as a Muslim majority, this is the first time the issue has proceeded so far. In a letter to UNESCO, Greece's culture minister said Erdogan's plan "rekindles national and religious fanaticism." US Secretary of State Mike Pompeo has angered Ankara with his statement that the monument is better left alone as a museum. The court will give its verdict within 15 days.
Botswana's conservation disaster: Botswana's Okavango Delta is strewn with the carcasses of dead elephants and scientists are at a loss to explain the mass deaths. In mid-May about a dozen elephants were reported dead; by the end of the month it was 169. That figure has now crossed 350, with conservationists warning the number is likely to be higher as carcasses can be difficult to spot in an aerial survey. Experts say more are likely to die, with live elephants appearing weak and shrunken and seen walking around in circles, an indication of neurological impairment. Samples have not yet been tested so it's not known what is causing the mass deaths. As tusks of the dead elephants have not been taken, poachers are being ruled out. Anthrax was initially considered the most likely cause but that has also been ruled out. Conservationists are concerned at Botswana's lack of urgency in getting the samples tested. "It's a conservation disaster – it speaks of a country that is failing to protect its most valuable resource," said Niall McCann, director of conservation at National Park Rescue, a UK-based charity. Between 10% and 12% of Botswana's GDP is attributable to ecotourism, only diamonds contribute more.
Mask up!
My Lord, it's common sense. That's what Tom Hanks has to say to those who "cannot find it in themselves" to just wear a mask.
Little did Lenin Gutierrez know that a simple question about a mask would raise such hell. On Monday, the Starbucks barista found himself at the receiving end of a diatribe when he inquired of a customer if she had a mask, something Starbucks requires its customers to wear. Amber Lynn Gilles' fury did not subside with the insults she hurled at him at the store; she later took to Facebook to vent further. In what one can only assume is to her utter dismay, her comments section was flooded with support for the barista, with some remarking that they wished they could give Gutierrez a tip. So Matt Cowan decided to facilitate just that. Upset that Gutierrez was "being bullied for doing the right thing," Cowan set up a GoFundMe so that those who wanted to tip the barista could do so. Contributions have crossed $100,000.
Unfortunately, a similar request to wear a mask did not stop at verbal abuses but got physical in India's Andhra Pradesh. When a colleague reminded him to wear a mask as part of India's coronavirus regulations, C Bhaskar, a senior state tourism department employee, abused, insulted and physically assaulted his female colleague. The police complaint against him reads that he "dragged her from her chair, caught hold of her hair and started beating her on her face and head with a stick pulled out from a wooden chair."
Meanwhile
Think you know what's best for your child? The UK's education secretary seems to think otherwise. Gavin Williamson has said that come September when schools reopen in the UK, parents had better have a "good reason" if they want to keep their kids away. And if found without one, they'll have to pay a fine.
Bear with me. A brown bear in Italy's Trentino region has been sentenced to death and animal rights activists aren't having it. About 15,000 people have already signed a petition to spare the animal and Sergio Costa, Italy's environment minister, has written to the governor of Trentino saying he is against the slaying of the bear, which attacked two men on a hiking trail last week. According to Italian regulations, bears that attack humans must be euthanized. Still, as DNA evidence is being gathered to identify the bear, animal rights groups are calling for a stay of the culling order at least until an investigation is carried out and more details emerge as to what led to the attack. Costa writes in his letter that it could have been a mother protecting her cubs.
Sanskrit, anyone? Sanskrit, once the spoken language of India and now heard only at religious ceremonies, is making a comeback in Karamana, a suburb of Thiruvananthapuram, thanks to an initiative rolled out in late 2018 to popularize the language. And Mani, a tea-stall owner, is doing his bit to bring the nearly dead language back to life. With the coronavirus slowing down business, Mani came up with the novel idea of offering a discount at his tea stall to anyone ordering in Sanskrit. Not only has business boomed, it's also been a welcome marketing move for the initiative.
Shed the pounds. That's what zookeepers are telling Mao, a female African elephant in Japan's Morioka Zoological Park. When Mao lost her appetite after her mate died, staff put her on a high-calorie diet in an attempt to maintain her weight. But it's more than maintain she did, she piled on quite a few extra pounds as well. Now officials are taking the help of visitors to the zoo to get her back in shape. Visitors are encouraging Mao to get moving and make the 60-meter journey to the fence of her enclosure by tempting her with bananas and apples. Once she attains her ideal weight, experts want to artificially inseminate her. A success story would mean Japan's first baby elephant born through artificial insemination. We're keeping our fingers crossed.
And over in the UK, Prime Minister Boris Johnson wants obese Britons to do the same, noting that a "fitter" population would be better equipped to deal with a health crisis.
Fancy a degree in ninja studies? There's a university for that. Located in central Japan, a region once filled with ninjas, Mie University in 2017 set up the world's first research center devoted to ninja studies and a year later started a graduate program. After spending two years at the university garnering martial arts skills and studying the ways of the ninja

–

those experts of silent assassination and espionage

–

Genichi Mitsuhashi has now become the only person in the world to hold a master's degree in ninja studies. The 45-year-old, who has adopted farming as a way of life to emulate the ways of the ninja, whose day job was tending the land, is keen to get a doctorate.
Want milk with that? South Koreans are in a frenzy over a green onion-flavored cereal that just hit the shelves. The limited-edition Chex cereal was one of the most searched keywords on major search portals in the weeks leading up to its July 1 launch. In a 2004 online poll by Kellogg's asking consumers for ideas for new cereals, green onion flavor, to almost everyone's surprise, was in huge demand. To quote someone from the cereal maker: "We were skeptical of making a cereal with the taste in the first place. But we made it after all, to show our respect for the support that people gave us 16 years ago."
I knew some good would come of that! Feeling calm and collected in the face of the raging pandemic? You've got those end-of-the-world movies to thank. Finally, there's proof that all those hours spent gorging on doomsday movies weren't a total waste. Psychologists say those apocalyptic movies have left us better equipped to deal with the coronavirus pandemic. A recent study of 310 volunteers suggests that while fans of horror movies are likely to be less distressed than most by the current crisis, fans of movies where society collapses when faced with flesh-eating zombies, invading aliens and contagious diseases sparing none, are mentally and practically better prepared to deal with the situation. So, if you haven't already, now is not a bad time to get caught up on those end-of-the-world movies.

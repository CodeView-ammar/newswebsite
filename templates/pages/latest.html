In this edition of Relevant & Irreverent, we're turning the spotlight on an artist who’s bringing Christmas joy in July and another who’s bringing Biden to Italian farms. We're also talking about bears, black and polar, and making the case for spell-checks.

In the spotlight

Photo: michaelgittes.com

Flower power brings Christmas joy in July: Every employee, from doctor to janitor to cafeteria worker, of Brooklyn's Interfaith Medical Center now owns an original Michael Gittes. When the pandemic was at its worst in the US, Los Angeles-based Gittes decided to create and gift an original painting to every employee of a hospital in New York, then the worst-hit state. Gittes picked Interfaith as it met his specifications for the perfect hospital for his donation, including that it was a non-profit hospital operating in an underserved community.

To pay homage to the hospital staff, the artist created 1,800 acrylic paintings, each representing a flower. Every painting in the "Strangers to No One" project was created using syringes. "In both cases, they are employing a syringe to help others heal," Gittes' manager said.

To the Interfaith staff, this was the artist's message: "You're loved by millions of people you'll never meet. You're not a stranger to anyone. And these flowers are from everyone."

Says Interfaith CFO Tracy Green of her staff's reaction when they received the paintings: "It was like Christmas."
Not a fan of paying income tax? Have 4 kids: Hungary has devised a plan to boost its dwindling numbers, but it's a plan that will benefit only the right kind of Hungarian, CBS' 60 Minutes says. With a population count of less than 10 million and falling, the Central European country under Prime Minister Viktor Orban in 2019 unveiled the Family Protection Action Plan – a $2.5 billion program aimed at improving the country's birthrate.

The plan offers families cash, loans and even subsidies to buy a minivan if they procreate. Just promising to have one child gets the couple a $30,000 loan. Rates are cut after two children and forgiven after three. If the couple can commit to at least four kids, the woman is exempted from paying income tax for life.

In a country that under Orban erected a $500 million fence along its border with Serbia and that the prime minister describes as an "illiberal democracy," strict eligibility criteria need to be met to benefit from the plan.

The couple must have enough savings of their own to be eligible; those who are gay, divorced, or not married are excluded.

"We must state that we do not want to be diverse and do not want to be mixed," Orban said in a speech last year.

Needless to say, only Hungarians need apply.

Polar bears bear brunt of climate change: Scientists made a grim prediction this week. In a study published in the journal Nature Climate Change, researchers warned that if global warming goes unchecked, polar bears will be nearly all gone by 2100. The study highlights the plight of polar bears when faced with shrinking sea ice and predicts that almost all of the 19 subpopulations of polar bears will be extinct by the end of the century if greenhouse gas emissions are not drastically cut down. For polar bears, their main habitat is sea ice where they live and hunt for food, a loss of sea ice would force them onto land and they would eventually starve. "There's not enough food on land to sustain a polar bear population," Peter Molnar, the lead author of the study, said.

You go, girl!

A forever Instagram moment. A video of a woman taking a selfie with a bear has gone viral this week. In Mexico's Chipinque Ecological Park, a curious bear approached a group of hikers and not only did the three women seen in the video keep their calm, one of them actually posed for a selfie while the bear was sniffing her hair! We're in awe, such poise and quick thinking too – after all it's not every day that you have a wild bear peeking over your shoulder, you've got to get an Instagram memory out of that! We're applauding these women's nerves of steel.

Photo: Talking Masks

Masks for the hearing impaired, courtesy a California teen. A high schooler in California is making the pandemic just a little bit easier for the deaf community. Isabella Appell, though not hard of hearing herself, realized early on the need for face masks that enable people with hearing difficulties to communicate. So, she started Talking Masks, a venture that makes masks with a clear plastic screen over the mouth thus allowing for lip reading. There is no charge for the masks, only a shipping cost and donations go directly to the Hearing Aid Project.
All-women Afghan robotics team unveils low-cost ventilator. The award-winning Afghan Girl's Robotics Team has just launched a low-cost ventilator to treat Covid-19 patients in a country whose healthcare system is reeling from decades of war and unrest. The team began work on the open-source ventilator in March and now, four months later, with the help of experts at Harvard University, the young women are finally ready with their ventilator. The lightweight device can operate on batteries for about 10 hours and costs approximately $700 to manufacture; a conventional ventilator costs about $20,000. There are only 800 ventilators in the South Asian country which has seen more than 35,000 coronavirus cases.

Meanwhile

Take a load off, Fanny and put the load right on me: Venice's gondolas are feeling the weight of heavy tourists and gondoliers have been forced to reduce the number of passengers on the da nolo gondolas – the ones that give tourists an experience of Venice's famous canals – to five from six. On the bigger gondola da parada that are used for ferrying people across the Grand Canal, capacity has been reduced to 12 from 14. "It's true that compared to 10 or 15 years ago, tourists weigh a bit more," said Andrea Balbi, president of Venice's gondoliers' association
Teachers take on Trump faithful: The Florida Education Association is approaching the courts to halt a state emergency order forcing schools to reopen in August. The association's Fedrick Ingram told CNN that some 23,000 children in the golden state have tested positive for the novel coronavirus. "It's unfortunate that we have a governor that is playing politics with children's lives," he said. Florida's Republican governor, Ron DeSantis, has been toeing the Trump line and been pushing to get children back into classrooms.

And over in Missouri, another Republican governor can't seem to understand what all the commotion about sending kids back to school is about. "These kids have got to get back to school," Governor Mike Parson said. "And if they do get Covid-19, which they will … they're going to get over it."

Biden's face carved on Italian field: US presidential hopeful Joe Biden has found a fan in Italian land artist Dario Gambarin. In a 27,000 square meter field in Castagnaro in the province of Verona, Gambarin, who for years has been showcasing his land art portraits in his northern Italian fields, on Sunday laid the finishing touches to his latest work. The artist wrote "jump and fly" beside the portrait, conveying his best wishes to the presidential candidate for a successful campaign. Former US President Barack Obama's portrait has also graced Gambarin's fields.
This is why you need to spell-check: A Long Island man came up with the idea of faking his own death to avoid jail time for two previous crimes. Robert Berger sent a phony death certificate to a Nassau County District Attorney prosecutor and but for a glaring spelling mistake might have pulled off the alleged fraud. District Attorney Madeline Singas said: "Submitting fake documents to prosecutors is always a bad idea … failure to use spell check made this alleged fraud especially glaring." The error: Registry was spelled as Regsitry. In addition to his previous sentences, Berger faces up to four years in prison if convicted in this latest scheme.

Delivery for Santino D'Antonio: A package from Colombia addressed to Santino D'Antonio, the fictional crime boss in the John Wick movie series, aroused the suspicion of cops at Milan Malpensa Airport. Closer scrutiny of the contents of the package revealed coffee beans stuffed with cocaine. Some 130 kilos of the drug was hidden inside more than 500 coffee beans, the cops said. Now that's an espresso we can't afford.

Meanwhile in Singapore, in a S$66,000 drug raid, police found 317g of ketamine hidden in a coconut. What next?
With a little help from my friends: That's how a new mother in India's remote Ladakh has been getting her breast milk to her son in Delhi. When Dorjey Palmo gave birth to her son on June 16, she knew something was wrong when he wouldn't suckle. On discovering that his esophagus and trachea were joined, the newborn was rushed to Delhi for emergency surgery. Although the operation was a success, the baby formula they were feeding him was not, so doctors suggested he be given his mother's milk. But with mom 1,000 kms away, that wasn't going to be easy. That's when Palmo's friends at Ladakh airport stepped in. "I am lucky to have some friends working at Ladakh airport, who assisted us in transporting the milk," the boy's father said. After more than 35 special deliveries of his mother's milk, the baby is now strong enough to travel home.
